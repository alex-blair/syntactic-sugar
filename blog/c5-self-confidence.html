Week 5 | 19 January 2017

Mindfulness vs self-awareness

This week we were asked to read Chapter 4 of Search Inside Yourself and think about the relationship between mindfulness and self-awareness described in the book.

  • Self-awareness: Recognising your emotions and their effects.
  • Self-assessment: Knowing your strengths and limits.
  • Self-confidence: A strong sense of your self-worth and capabilities.
Chade-Meng Tan describes a linear relationship between the above factors - that strong emotional awareness leads to more accurate self-assessment, which in turn leads to higher confidence. In the end, he notes that self-awareness is essentially mindfulness. Both are trainable, both can be developed with practice.

Becoming more self-aware

The book describes some ways of enhancing self-awareness. One of these is keeping a journal. I've done this before, and I always find it quite relaxing. Sometimes I start writing because I've got a problem that seems impossible to solve, and by the end I realise the whole thing's not as difficult as I thought. This metaphor from the book captures it nicely: "the moment you can see a raging river, it means you are already rising above it" (p. 78). By the time I've accurately captured something in words, I usually have a much better understanding of it and what needs to be done.

"My Emotions Are Not Me"

This heading sounds like something you'd mutter to yourself after discovering someone has used a metal spoon on your best non-stick pan. It's one of the key points in Chapter 4. Basically, it comes down to the idea that "emotions are simply what you feel, not who you are" (p. 90). I guess that means that when you find the pan, and notice your frustration levels rising, you should realise that what you're feeling is just a temporary state, and learn to move past it.
